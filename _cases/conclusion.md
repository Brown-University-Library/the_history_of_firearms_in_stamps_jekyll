**Stamps are one way that a nation’s government tells the public, and people in other nations, about national history.**

The story of American stamps depicting firearms includes never-ending progress. The story begins with primitive European matchlocks, soon to be replaced by more reliable flintlocks, which were better suited for American conditions. The development of mass production from interchangeable parts, strongly supported by the federal government, started early in the 19th century. Inventions such as the percussion cap and smokeless powder facilitated major improvements in firearms design. Some of the fruits of the technological advances were the revolvers from Colt and other manufacturers, beginning in the 1830s; then, breechloaders and lever-action repeaters, beginning shortly before the Civil War. Although repeaters became common, single-shot rifles remained important throughout the century. Before the end of the 19th century, pump action shotguns, bolt action rifles, and semiautomatic rifles had come on stage, and all of them would be widely used in the 20th century. 

The greatest period of stamps with firearms was the Bicentennial decade of the 1970s, with many stamps related to the American Revolution.

Many other stamps depicting Americans with firearms were produced starting with the 1924 Huguenot-Walloon stamp (honoring French religious refugees who fled to America) through the end of the 20th century.

In the 21st century, there have been no U.S. postage stamps depicting firearms. This gap is reminiscent of 23-year gap between the Pony Express (1869) and the Westward Expansion series (1892), and the gap between that series and the 1924 Huguenots.
