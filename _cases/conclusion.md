Stamps are one way for a nation’s government to tell national stories to a nation’s public,
and also to people in other countries who receive mail from that nation. The prime message
conveyed by the American firearms stamps is not about American firearms, but about the
American people, portraying them as active defenders of their liberties, and energetic settlers
of the continent. To the extent that foes are portrayed, they are never shown in a negative
light, but instead presented as valiant warriors, such as the British, the Confederates, and
Native Americans.

Sometimes, individual stamps are conceived as part of a series, such as the Bicentennial
stamps or the Celebrate the Century panes. Occasionally, stamps consciously look back to
much earlier stamps, as in the 1998 reissue of the 1898 westward expansion stamps, the
Bicentennial new versions of American Revolution stamps from the Sesquicentennial, or the
America 250 “Battlefields” stamps for the Sesquicentennial and the Bicentennial.
Another story appears when stamps are viewed, as in most of this exhibit, in chronological
order of the events depicted, rather than in order of when they were issued. Here, the
firearms depicted on American stamps tell a story of continual progress, beginning with
primitive European matchlocks giving way to the more reliable flintlocks, which were better
suited for the conditions in America.

In the nineteenth century, the development of mass production using interchangeable parts
further hastened the pace of innovation, and inventions like the percussion cap and
smokeless powder facilitated major improvements in design. Some of the fruits of such
technological advances included the revolvers from Colt and other manufacturers, beginning
in the 1830s and breech-loaders and lever-action repeaters shortly before the Civil War,
though single-shot rifles remained important throughout the century. Before the end of the
nineteenth century, pump-action shotguns, bolt-action rifles and semiautomatic rifles
appeared on the stage, and all of them were widely used in the twentieth century.
The Bicentennial decade of the 1970s, and its many stamps related to the American
Revolution, marked the greatest period of stamps depicting firearms. More broadly, firearms
stamps were common in the last three quarters of the twentieth century, rare in the
nineteenth, and did not appear in the twenty-first until the nation’s 250th
anniversary, in 2026.

There are other things that have appeared on American stamps more often than firearms —
for example horses, flowers, or flags. Nevertheless, no other nation has produced so many
firearms stamps, and the plentitude of firearms in American stamps is a reflection of the
importance of firearms in American history and culture.
