This stamp declares the National Guard to be “The oldest military organization in the U.S.A.” The oldest American military organizations were the militias of the colonies, and so the Guard was claiming descent from them, in a moral sense.

During the Civil War, the Union’s marksmanship training of soldiers was often cursory or worse. After the war, a new sort of independent volunteer militias arose; they usually called themselves the “National Guard.” As the nineteenth century progressed, National Guard units received official state recognition, financial support, and training—in exchange for state control.

The National Guard developed a symbiotic relationship with the National Rifle Association. The NRA’s 1871 corporate charter from New York State included the purpose “to promote the introduction of a system of aiming drill and target firing among the National Guard of New York and the militia of other states.” 

Later, after the Spanish-American War, Congress passed the Militia Act of 1903. It defined the National Guard as the “organized militia” of the United States. The “unorganized militia” “of the Untied States” is “all able-bodied males at least 17 years of age” and under 45 [10 U.S. Code §246](https://codes.findlaw.com/us/title-10-armed-forces/10-usc-sect-246/).
