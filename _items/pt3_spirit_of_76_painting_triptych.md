These stamps are a triptych—that is, three images that are part of a single complete unit. Musicians were important elements of military units. The fife and drum kept time on long marches and inspired soldiers. Crucially, they also conveyed orders during combat, as the drums—unlike voices at a distance—could be heard above the din of battle.

The triptych comes from the main part of Archibald Willard’s 1876 painting “The Spirit of 76.” Look at how well the stamps capture the fine detail of the painting, especially the emotions on the faces of the three musicians.

The drummer on the left is too young for combat duty, but boys his age often served in accessory roles, including musical ones.

Many stamps employ metonymy—where a part of an object is recognized to stand for the entire object. For example, when you say “my wheels,” people understand that you mean your entire automobile. Here, the fragment of the widely-known painting is understood to stand for the entire painting.
