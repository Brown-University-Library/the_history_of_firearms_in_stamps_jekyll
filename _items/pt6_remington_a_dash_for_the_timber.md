Frederic Remington’s paintings and sculptures are and were
among the greatest portrayals of the Old West. This stamp is an
excerpt from “A Dash for the Timber.” The stamp is part of the
“American Illustrators” souvenir series.

The man on the left on the black horse is firing a carbine. During
the post-Civil War period, repeating carbines became very
common in the West. Some could fire 18 shots without
reloading. The most popular was the [Winchester Model 1873
lever-action rifle](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/the-american-west-1850-to-1900/case-20-colt-winchester/winchester-model-1866-lever-action-rifle.aspx).

![Winchester Model 1866 lever-action repeating rifle. The rifle features a 24-inch dark octagonal barrel, a full-length tubular magazine underneath, a distinctive polished brass receiver with a side loading gate, a dark walnut fore-end, and a walnut buttstock with a crescent brass buttplate.](../img/itemimgs/winchester_model_1866_lever_action_rifle.jpg)

The man on the right has a revolver. With a typical ammunition
capacity of 5 or 6 rounds, revolvers were also good guns for
mounted men, although they were less accurate at a distance
than carbines, since carbines have longer barrels.

The pursuing Indians on horseback are difficult to make out
clearly, but the man in the front has some sort of gun with a very
long barrel. This is the one of two American stamps that depicts
an Indian with a firearm. The other is a stamp honoring
Geronimo, below.
