The bicentennial of the War of 1812 was honored in stamps  issued in 2012-15.

This scene is where The Star Spangled Banner was born. On  Sept. 13-14, 1814, the British Navy bombarded Ft. McHenry,  Maryland. Across the Baltimore harbor, Francis Scott Key was  being held prisoner by the British. From his cell, he watched Ft.  McHenry perseveres, and the star spangled banner still waves. The  poem he wrote was later declared the national anthem, in 1931.

![A U.S. Springfield Model 1795 Type I flintlock musket, featuring a long dark wood stock, smoothbore metal barrel, prominent flintlock mechanism, and an iron ramrod](../img/itemimgs/u_s_springfield_model_1795_flintlock_musket_type_i.webp)

The soldiers on the right carry the standard U.S. Army muskets of  the time, manufactured at the armories in Springfield, Massachusetts, or Harpers Ferry, Virginia. The photo shows a [1795 Springfield Musket](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/a-prospering-new-republic-1780-to-1860/case-7-early-american-military-arms/us-springfield-model-1795-flintlock-musket-type-i.aspx). Patterned after the French Charleville, it  was the first type of firearm produced by the Springfield Armory.

The lessons of the War of 1812 led President James Madison,  Secretary of War James Monroe to convince Congress to fund a  major research and development program to increase the  quality, pace, and affordability of American arms manufacture.  The federal research program succeeded; the skills developed for  mass production of firearms with interchangeable parts became a  foundation of the industrial revolution in America, and of American  participation in global manufacturing.
