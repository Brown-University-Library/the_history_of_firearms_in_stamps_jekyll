David Crockett (1786-1836) spent most of his life in Tennessee, except  for four years in the District of Columbia when he was elected U.S.  Representative. As a Tennessean, he stuck with the American Long Rifle  (a/k/a Pennsylvania-Kentucky rifle).

The metal inset of the rifle’s stock covered a hollow area for storage of  cleaning tools or other supplies. The plainsmen on the far side of the  Mississippi did not want this feature on their rifles, because the metal  could catch and reflect sunlight, perhaps revealing their location to a  distant observer. In Davy Crockett’s dense Tennessee woods, sunlight  was much less of a risk.

Crockett wears a coonskin cap, the iconic hat of the American  frontiersman. Coonskin caps came back into fashion--at least for  American boys--thanks to the 1954 Disney 5-hour miniseries, “Davy  Crockett: King of the Wild Frontier.”

In 1835, Crockett went to Texas to help that new nation fight for its  independence from the Mexican military dictator Santa Anna. He died  defending the Alamo, on Mar. 6, 1836.
