This military recruiting poster for the war became a
stamp as part of the Postal Service’s “Celebrate the
Century” series at the end of the twentieth century.
Each decade was honored with a series of stamps
portraying its popular culture and achievements.

![U.S. Smith-Corona Model 1903A3 bolt-action rifle. The military firearm features a full-length dark walnut stock without finger grooves, a parkerized receiver ring, a blued turn-bolt handle, an aperture rear sight mounted directly onto the back of the receiver, and a full-length wooden handguard exposing just the tip of the 24-inch barrel.](../img/itemimgs/u_s_smith_corona_model_1903a3_bolt_action_rifle.webp)

During World War II, and in World War I, Smith-
Corona turned its typewriter factories into rifle
factories, and produced the [Smith-Corona Model 1903
bolt action rifle](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/wwii,-korea,-vietnam-and-beyond-1940-to-present/case-39-wwii-the-united-states/us-smith-corona-model-1903a3-bolt-action-rifle.aspx) --a variant of
the Springfield model 1903. This is a 1903-A3, made in WWII; in WWI the
sights were flat, and in front of the bolt.

Smith-Corona’s ability to switch into firearms
manufacture was not surprising. The production of
typewriters had been one of the fruits of “the American
System” of mass production of complex tools with
interchangeable parts–a system that began with
firearms, and later was applied to other tools.
