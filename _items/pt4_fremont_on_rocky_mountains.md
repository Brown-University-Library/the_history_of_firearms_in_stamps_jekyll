Captain John C. Frémont led five expeditions of discovery in the Rocky  Mountains in the 1840s and 1850s. In 1856, he was the first presidential nominee of the Republican party, running under the motto “Free Speech, Free Press, Free Soil, Free Men, Frémont and Victory.” The Republican platform was no expansion of slavery into the territories, which should be free soil for industrious free men.

This stamp, and the next one, were issued in 1898, the year of Trans Mississippi and International Exposition, which promoted development of  the West. Originally designed as two-color stamps (colored borders,  vignettes in black), they were issued in monochrome because of labor  shortages arising from the Spanish-American war, which began in April  1898. The 1998 reissue pictured here did use the original two-color images.

![U.S. Hall Model 1836 breechloading percussion carbine. It features a full-length dark wood stock, a smooth metallic gray barrel with two barrel bands, a distinct pivoting breechblock mechanism behind the barrel, and a dark metal trigger guard.](../img/itemimgs/u_s_hall_model_1836_breechloading_percussion_carbine.webp)

The expedition soldier on the lower right may be carrying a standard U.S. Army rifle of the time, the [U.S. Hall model 1836](https://www.nramuseum.org/the-museum/the-galleries/the-prospering-new-republic/case-31-the-age-of-industry/us-hall-model-1836-breechloading-percussion-carbine.aspx). It uses percussion ignition.

The 1898 stamp was the second time the U.S. flag appeared on a stamp,  the first being in 1869 (Scott #121). Flags on stamps were controversial, since they would be desecrated, in a sense, by cancellation.
