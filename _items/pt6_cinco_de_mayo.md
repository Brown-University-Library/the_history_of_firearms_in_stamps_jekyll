The Cinco de Mayo celebration was created in 1862
by Mexican-Americans to commemorate a Mexican
victory against the invading French army of Napoleon
III.
The Franco-Mexican war continued until 1867, and
American firearms played an important role in the
liberation of Mexico. When the French occupied
Mexico City, Mexican President Benito Juárez set up
a resistance movement in northern Mexico. There, he
ordered 1,000 [Winchester Model 1866 carbines](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/the-american-west-1850-to-1900/case-20-colt-winchester/winchester-model-1866-lever-action-rifle.aspx) in .44
caliber, to be delivered to Monterrey, along with 500
cartridges per gun.

![Winchester Model 1866 lever-action repeating rifle. The rifle features a 24-inch dark octagonal barrel, a full-length tubular magazine underneath, a distinctive polished brass receiver with a side loading gate, a dark walnut fore-end, and a walnut buttstock with a crescent brass buttplate.](../img/itemimgs/winchester_model_1866_lever_action_rifle.jpg)

The Juárez forces paid $57,000 in
silver coin. “R.M.” – for “Republic of Mexico” – was
inscribed on the frames of the carbines. Today,
“Juarez Winchesters” are very valuable collectors
items. The Winchester 1866 was the state of the art
rifle of its time, a fast-shooting repeater with ample
ammunition capacity.
