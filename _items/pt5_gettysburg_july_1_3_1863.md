This stamp depicts action at Gettysburg from the Confederate side. Modern  “smokeless” gunpowder was not invented until 1884, so the firearms and the artillery  on both sides were using traditional black powder. Because black powder burns  relatively inefficiently, it quickly generates a smoky haze, obscuring the view of the  front lines.

![U.S. Spencer Model 1863 lever-action repeating carbine. The firearm features a dark wooden buttstock and matching fore-end, a short blued steel barrel, a dark metal receiver, an external hammer, and a distinctive curved trigger guard lever.](../img/itemimgs/u_s_spencer_lever_action_repeating_carbine.webp)

The photo is an [1863 Spencer lever action carbine](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/a-nation-asunder-1861-to-1865/case-14-union-carbines/us-spencer-lever-action-repeating-carbine.aspx), of the type used by the Michigan  cavalry at Gettysburg. The trigger guard is attached to a lever; to reload another  round, the user simply pulls the lever down and then pushes it back up. Although  invented in the 17th century , lever actions were expensive and rare in America until  shortly before the Civil War. A 7-shot repeater, the Spencer gave me a big advantage to  any soldier fortunate enough to have one. 

Over a hundred thousand Spencers were used by the Union during the war,  particularly by cavalry . President Lincoln, a gun aficionado, personally tested one on  the White House lawn. The U.S. The Ordnance Department bought them as fast as the  Spencer company could make them. However, the federal armory at Springfield,  Massachusetts, and its associated contractors could not suddenly shift their  production lines to start making Spencer repeaters, which are internally complex. The  federal manufacturing system was not even able to keep up with the needs for  making the much simpler single-shot muzzleloading rif led musket, which had been a  state of the art gun in the 1840s. Not until two years into the war was the Union able  to supply these guns to the entire Army .
