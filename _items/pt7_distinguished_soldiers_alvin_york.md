This stamp and the next one (Audie Murphy) are two of the four stamps in the “Distinguished Soldiers” series. The other two are WWI General John L. Hines and WWII General Omar N. Bradley. 

Alvin York and Audie Murphy were the greatest American marksmen of World War I and World War II, respectively.

Alvin York grew up poor in rural Kentucky. With his family’s very old cap-and-ball muzzle-loading rifle, he had to hit a squirrel or wild turkey with a precise shot to the head, so as not to waste any meat.

On the morning of October 8, 1918, in France’s Argonne Forest, York’s unit was searching for “the Lost Battalion,” which had been surrounded by Germans. When York’s patrol found a German camp, York shot one German and the rest surrendered. Suddenly, German machine guns in the nearby hills opened fire, and nine Americans were killed or wounded. York, with his Enfield rifle, picked off the machine gunners one by one. When his rifle ran out of ammunition, the six remaining Germans charged. He took each of them down with his Colt .45 pistol. While there have always been disputes about the details of the battle and the role played by the other American soldiers, York’s superlative marksmanship was undeniable. York and the seven other able-bodied American soldiers began marching several dozen German prisoners back to American lines. On the way, they encountered two other groups of Germans, and bluffed them into surrendering.

York and his men brought in 132 German prisoners. They had killed 25 Germans and knocked 35 German machine guns out of action. Marshall Foch, the French commander, called York’s feat “the greatest thing accomplished by any private soldier in all the armies of Europe.” He was carrying a 1917 Winchester version of the Enfleld, which is shown close-up in the slide for the Second Battle of the Marne
