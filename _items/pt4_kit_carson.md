Around the 1820s, the Mountain Men started heading to the  Rocky Mountains and beyond. These itinerant traders established  good relationships with Indians, and traded American goods, such  as ammunition and gunpowder, for peltry--especially beaver and,  later, buffalo robes. They were constantly armed for protection  from animals and from tribes who were hostile to the tribes that  the Mountain Men had befriended.

Many Mountain Men married Indian wives, sometimes  polygamously, and their families were cultural intermediaries  between the Americans and the Indians. 
Foremost of the mountain men was Kit Carson. He guided  expeditions, led the U.S. Army in warfare against the Utes in the  1840s, and thereafter won their respect for his fair dealings with  them as an Indian agent.

In the stamp, he carries his [Hawken rifle](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/a-prospering-new-republic-1780-to-1860/case-9-the-plains-rifle/william-hawken-full-stock-percussion-conversion-rifle.aspx) and a powder horn, and  wears a buffalo hat. He also carried a pair of Colt revolvers.

Americans crossing the Mississippi did not prefer the American Long Rifle, which had been perfect for shooting small targets in  dense Eastern woods. On the plains, a larger caliber was needed  for game such as buffalo, grizzly bear, or elk. Plainsmen also  needed a shorter gun for use on horseback.

St. Louis, “the gateway to the West,” would the manufacturing  center for these new rifles, of which the Hawken was the most.
