Congress in July 1862 authorized the enlistment of “persons  of African descent” in the military, thereby returning to the  policy that had been followed by most state militias during  the American Revolution and before.

By the end of the war, the regiments of “United States  Colored Troops” comprised about a seventh of the U.S.  Army.

At Petersburg, these troops could have won the Civil War  then and there, according to a plan by Gen. Ambrose  Burnside, who was a great supporter of those troops.  Unfortunately, his plan was overridden by General Meade,  who insisted that the decisive charge be made by white  troops, who happened to be led by a notoriously unreliable  officer. As a result, the siege of Petersburg, and the war,  dragged on until April 1865.

The long arm features a dark wood stock stretching nearly the length of the barrel, a steel barrel secured by three metal bands, and a percussion lock mechanism near the trigger.](../img/itemimgs/barnett_model_1853_enfield_percussion_rifle_musket.webp)

Second only to Springfield rifles, the British-made [1853 Enfield Rifle Musket](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/a-nation-asunder-1861-to-1865/case-17-civil-war-imported-arms/barnett-model-1853-enfield-percussion-rifle-musket.aspx) was the most common firearm on both  side of the Civil War.
