Whereas English colonists were mostly left to fend for  themselves (and to govern themselves), French and Spanish  colonization was directed by the monarchs and was carried out by the military forces of those nations.

![Mid-16th century French civilian sporting wheellock rifle, circa 1560, with long rifled barrel, wooden stock, and intricate internal wheel-lock mechanism.](../img/itemimgs/french_wheellock_rifle.jpg)

Here, Nicolet’s 1634 expedition to Green Bay, Wisconsin, would  have carried the best contemporary firearms of the French  military. This [French wheellock](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/ancient-firearms-1350-to-1700/case-3-old-guns-in-the-new-world/french-wheellock-rifle.aspx) was a major advance over  matchlocks; it was ignited by sparks from flint striking steel. The  photo depicts a wheellock’s complicated “lock.” It created a  shower of sparks to ignite the gunpowder in the priming pan.

Note that the Indians have spears and bows, but not firearms.  Early European colonists were often welcomed by nearby Indians  and mutually beneficial trading relationships quickly developed.  The trade goods that Europeans desired most were peltry,  whereas the Indians most wanted firearms, as well as edged  weapons made of steel. A friendly trading relationship with the  colonists gave a tribe a huge military advantage over other tribes  that lacked trade and firearms. The French were adept at using  the arms trade to build relationships with native allies.
