These saving stamps were not used for postage, but they were sold by the Post Office. Starting in 1911, Postal Saving stamps could be used for credit in postal savings accounts, the small bank accounts that the Post Office operated at the time. During World War I, a quantity of these stamps could be redeemed for a bond or similar debt instrument to finance the U.S. war effort. The program was discontinued after the war, and then reintroduced in 1942, with the new Minuteman design shown here.

With some variations in background, the Minuteman savings stamp was used through 1957. New designs, based on the U.S. flag, began in 1958, and continued until the end of the U.S. Savings Stamp program in 1970. Denominations were 10, 25, or 50 cents, or one dollar.

During World War II, every means for Americans to finance the massive debt necessary for war spending was important. After the war, Savings Stamps were often used to encourage thrift in school children. A booklet filled with $18.75 in stamps could be exchanged for a $25 savings bond, which was a promise by the U.S. government to pay the bearer $25 at a specified future date.
