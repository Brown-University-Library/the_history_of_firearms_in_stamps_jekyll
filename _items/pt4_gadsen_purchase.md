Following the Mexican War, the United States of Mexico sold much of the American  Southwest to the United States. Then in 1853 the U.S. and Mexico negotiated the  Gadsden Purchase. This stamp commemorates the centenary . A key purpose of the  Gadsden Purchase was to acquire land that would be suitable for a transcontinental  railroad. The Southern Pacific Railroad’s Texas-to-California “Sunset Route” would  open in 1883.

The man on the left t front of the stage coach appears to be “riding shotgun”--with a firearm ready for defense against robbers or hostile Indians. 

![Civil War-era Sharps Model 1853 single-shot percussion carbine, showing its distinctive slanting breech, wooden stock, metal barrel, and side hammer.](../img/itemimgs/sharps_model_1853_single_shot_breechloading_percussion_carbine.webp)

Perhaps the horseman on the right is carrying a new [Sharps Model 1853 breechloading percussion carbine](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/a-nation-asunder-1861-to-1865/case-15-union-muskets-and-rifles/sharps-model-1853-single-shot-breechloading-percussion-carbine.aspx). (A carbine is a short rifle.) 

Introduced in 1850, the Sharps was a breechloader; it is loaded from the back of the  gun, near the trigger, like all modern firearms. Before 1850, most American guns had  been muzzleloaders, which load from the front of the barrel. Breechloaders had been  around for centuries, but until the American Industrial Revolution (which was initiated  by f irearms manuf acture), breechloaders had been too expensive e for most people,  
because their internal small parts are more intricate. Mass production of  interchangeable parts solved the problem

The Sharps’ breechloading mechanism made it possible for even a novice to fire 9  shots a minute. Particularly popular with pioneer families heading West, the Sharps  was a superb long-range gun, and remained in common use for decades afterward.
