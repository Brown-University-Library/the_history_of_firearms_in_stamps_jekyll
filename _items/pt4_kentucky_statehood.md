The 1774 settlement Fort Harrod had led to much more immigration. By 1792, Kentucky was populous enough to  become a State, separate from her mother Virginia.  
The stamp depicts the widely-held view of eastern Kentucky as a  bountiful land. It was also a contested land. American population  growth, a product of high levels of fecundity and of immigration, created population pressures.  
Although much of the land transfers from Indians to the  immigrants was based on formal sales, there was often bribery,  deception, and coercion. Some Indians purported to sell lands  that were not theirs, but which belonged to other tribes. Some  of the land-takers were equally or more dishonest.
