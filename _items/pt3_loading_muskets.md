The Caribbean island democracy of Grenada issued its own stamps to commemorate the bicentennial.

The diversity of the clothing of these militiamen is historically accurate. The militiamen generally had to supply themselves with their clothing and their firearms.

There are other Grenada stamps, not pictured here, depicting firearms in the American Revolution. One (Grenada no. 717, issued 1776), in the same style as the previous three, shows an American using a musket to club a British soldier who was trying to prevent access to a Liberty Pole, a revolutionary symbol. A stamp in 1975 series (no. Grenada 632) commemorates “The Shot Heard Round the World” with three rustically-dressed Americans and their muskets.

A 1775 stamp (Grenada-Grenadines no. 94) reproduces a painting of the death of Dr. Joseph Warren at the Battle of Bunker Hill. It is similar to U.S. no. 1564, previously depicted. The stamp issued in the name of Grenada and the Grenadines; the southern part of the Grenadines island chain is part of the nation of Grenada.
