The sheet reproduces the famous 1851 painting by Emanuel Leutze. Within the sheets are five individual postage stamps.

After the defeats at Brooklyn and Manhattan, the discouraged Americans were on the brink of giving up the war. Many of the soldiers in Washington’s army had enlistments that were about to expire. On Christmas night, 1776, General Washington led the crossing of the Delaware River, for a surprise attack on the British at Trenton the next morning. The victory at Trenton restored American morale and kept the Revolution alive.

The painting is symbolic, and not strictly realistic. Washington and the boats were crossing in the middle of the night, not at dawn. The icy river is more typical the Rhine River in Germany (from where Leutze had emigrated) rather than the Delaware. The boat is smaller and more crowded than the one that Washington used. Future President James Monroe is depicted holding an American flag, but that particular flag did not come into use until the next year. (Even though it is also depicted on the 1776 White Plains stamp.)

All of the above were deliberate choices by painter Leutze, and not errors: The scene is set in the dawn to represent the dawning of democracy. The crowed boat, including a Black, a Scotsman, an Indian, and a woman, shows Americans working together, recognizing they are all in the same boat, literally and figuratively. When Leutze, a fervent abolitionist, was painting in 1851, the United States was in a crisis over the slavery issue; the flag reminds the viewers of 1851 to return to America’s founding ideals. For more, see Meir Soloveichik, _The Deeper Meaning of ‘Washington Crossing the Delaware’_, Wall St. Journal, Feb. 17, 2024.
