The first repeating rifle adopted by the U.S. Army as a standard infantry weapon was the
Norwegian Krag–Jørgensen rifle in 1892. But in the Spanish-American War, it was proven to
the German-made Mauser rifles of the Spanish. So the Army adopted a rifle based on the
Mauser, the 1903 Springfield. It remained the standard Army rifle for decades, and many of
the rifles were still in use in World War II, especially in the early period of U.S. involvement.
The rifle’s cartridge, the venerable .30-’06 became and still is the leading American cartridge
for hunting deer and larger game. It was invented in 1906, to fit a barrel bore that is
30/100ths of an inch wide.

As the long bayonet indicates, troops were expected to use the gun in shooting at German
soldiers in distant trenches, and in hand-to-hand combat when attempting to capture an
enemy trench.

The stamp is based on Captain Harvey Dunn’s drawing of American soldiers at the Second
Battle of the Marne. In Russia, the Bolsheviks (a totalitarian communist sect) had seized
power in an October 1917 coup, and promptly surrendered to Germany–which had secretly
been financing the Bolsheviks. German troops from the Eastern Front were shipped west as
Germany attempted to win the war in France with a final offensive. Near the Marne River, in
France, the German offensive began on July 15, 1918, and continued until August 6.
Although initially successful, the German offensive was blunted, and the Allied counterattack
began a relentless Allied advance that resulted in Germany’s surrender on November 11.

In bolt action rifles, the user reload by pulling the knobbed handle up, back, forward, and
then down. The action ejects the empty metal ammunition shell and loads a fresh round of
ammuntion into the firing chamber.
