Partly because of the early limitations of stamp printing, the first  American stamp (George Washington, 1851) and every stamp for  decades tended to be simple pictures of the profiles or faces of  famous Americans, such as Washington or Benjamin Franklin. An  11 stamp pictorial series in 1869 was not well received, and the  Post Office returned to just showing famous individuals. That  changed in 1892, during the global celebration of Columbus’s  voyage to the Western Hemisphere. The Post Office issued 7  extra-wide commemorative stamps of vignettes from Columbus’  life. The Post Office then reverted to simple portraits until 1898,  when the Westward Expansion commemoratives were issued.

In this 1898 stamp, U.S. Army troops guard a wagon train heading  West. Wagons usually traveled in a group, for collective security.  The wagon train here had the unusual good fortune of a military  escort for at least part of its journey.
