The most iconic American photo of World War II
shows Marines raising the flag atop Mount Suribachi,
on the Pacific Island of Iwo Jima.

The 36-day battle for the fortified island in Feb.-Mar.
1945 caused heavy casualties on both sides. Iwo
Jima was the first of the Japanese empire’s home
islands to be captured.

![World War II-era U.S. military Stevens Model 620 pump-action shotgun. The firearm features a dark blued receiver, a grooved walnut slide handle, an underlying tubular magazine, and a smooth walnut buttstock with a checkered plastic buttplate.](../img/itemimgs/u_s_stevens_model_620_slide_action_shotgun.webp)

Although rifles were the standard infantry weapon,
shotguns, such as the [U.S. Stevens Model 620 Slide
Action Shotgun](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/wwii,-korea,-vietnam-and-beyond-1940-to-present/case-39-wwii-the-united-states/us-stevens-model-620-slide-action-shotgun.aspx), also saw extensive use. On the
Pacific Island, Japanese soldiers had built fortified
positions, sometimes in caves. Shotguns were
necessary for the close-quarters fighting against
determined foes.
