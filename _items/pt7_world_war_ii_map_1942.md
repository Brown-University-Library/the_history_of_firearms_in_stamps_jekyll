In 1991-95, the Postal Service issued commemorative panes about the United States in World War II. The pane for 1941 did not depict any firearms. This 1942 pane includes the Battle of Guadalcanal, which began with the Marine landing on Aug. 7, 1942. The Japanese had seized the island, part of the Solomons, in July, and began building an airfield that could be used to strike the New Hebrides, New Caledonia, and New Guinea. Fighting on the island lasted half a year and was a turning point in the War in the Pacific.

Most infantry in World War II fought with bolt action rifles, as in World War I. The U.S. was the leader at transitioning to semiautomatic rifles, which have a higher rate of fire. For civilians, semiautomatic rifles had debuted in 1884, first produced by Mannlicher. But it took decades of experience with civilian semiautomatics to develop a semiauto rifle that could stand up to the rough conditions of infantry use in every global environment.

While the U.S. Army officially adopted the semiautomatic M1 Garand in 1936, many older rifles were still in use when the U.S. entered World War II. At Guadalcanal, some Marines had 1903 Springfield bolt-actions, while others had M1 Garands. In the hard fighting on Guadalcanal, Marines overwhelmingly preferred the Garand, leading the Marine Corps to make it the standard service rifle.
