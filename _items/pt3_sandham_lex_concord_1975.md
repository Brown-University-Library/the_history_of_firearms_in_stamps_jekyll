The stamp is based on the same Sandman painting as the previous stamp, from 1925. It was  issued on the 50th anniversary of the old stamp and the 200th anniversary of the battle.  Stamp printing had vastly improved during the half-century, including with the introduction  of multiple colors in the late 1960s.

The British army carried the newest model of the  Brown Bess musket. The men of New England—-and  some women, who also fought on April 19, 1775— typically had versions of the Brown Bess, such as  leftovers from the French & Indian War.  

![A circa 1775 American Committee of Safety flintlock musket. Modeled after the British "Brown Bess," this .75 caliber, muzzle-loading firearm features a plain, dark-stained wooden stock, a long metal barrel, and a standard, unmarked flintlock mechanism.](../img/itemimgs/committee_of_safety_flintlock_musket.webp)

Pictured is a [Committee of Safety flintlock musket](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/road-to-american-liberty-1700-to-1780/case-4-shot-heard-round-the-world/committee-of-safety-flintlock-musket.aspx). These firearms were typically patterned after other  guns, especially the Brown Bess. They were manufactured at the request of the local Patriot organizations, which usually called themselves the  “Committee of Safety.”
