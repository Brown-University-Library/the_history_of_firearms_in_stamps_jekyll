This stamp is a portion of a watercolor drawn at the 1781 siege of Yorktown by Jean
Baptiste Antoine de Verger, a lieutenant in the French Army that was fighting alongside
the Americans. Popularly known at “Four Soldiers,” the painting depicts the diversity of
soldiers in the Continental Army.

On the left is an infantryman of the First Rhode Island Regiment, nicknamed the “Black
Regiment” because of the skin color of most of its volunteers. The anchor and the blue
and white plumes of his regimental cap evoke Rhode Island’s nautical orientation. He
was probably formerly enslaved and enlisted in exchange for freedom.

The other man in the stamp, on the right, is from the Second Canadian Regiment,
composed of Canadians who joined in the 1776-76 attempt by the Continental Congress
to liberate Canada from British colonial rule. Many of the French-speaking soldiers in the
Second Canadian continued to fight on the American side throughout the war. Like the
R.I. man, the Franco-Canadian-American bears a Charleville musket, of the type that the
French supplied in great quantities to their American allies.

The full painting by de Verger includes two more men. The frontiersman, wearing a
fringed hunting coat, carries an American Long Rifle. On the far right, the artilleryman
holds a port-fire, a slow-burning match to ignite artillery.
