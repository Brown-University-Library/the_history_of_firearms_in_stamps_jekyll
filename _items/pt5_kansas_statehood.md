This stamp commemorates the centenary of the admission of the State of Kansas to the Union on January 29, 1861. The Civil War that would engulf the nation had been going in Kansas ever since 1854. That year, the Kansas-Nebraska Act had created the Kansas Territory, and provided that the issue of slavery in Kansas would be settled by a vote of the people. Pro-slavery and anti-slavery settlers poured in, and they were soon at war, creating competing territorial governments. At times, the pro-slavery side was aided by the federal forces, since Presidents Franklin Pierce and James Buchanan were Democrats.

After Abraham Lincoln was elected President in November 1860, the process of Southern secession began. By late January, so many slave state delegations had walked out of Congress that Republicans had solid control of both chambers. Thus, it was possible to admit Kansas to the Union as a free state.

The settler couple in the lower right is carrying a Sharps rifle. A breechloader, the Sharps used the then-new metallic ammunition cartridge, similar to modern ammunition, and could fire 9 shots a minute. Large numbers had been shipped to Kansas by the New England Emigrant Aid Company, which was organized by Boston minister Henry Ward Beecher to support anti-slavery emigrants. To prevent theft in transit, the tops of the crates of rifles were covered with Bibles. Hence, the Sharps rifles became known as “Beecher Bibles.”
