Another Grenada stamp honors Crispus Attucks, who in a sense was the first American to die in the Revolution. In response to the Boston Tea Party, the British government had put Boston under military occupation. The redcoats were very unpopular with the townspeople. On March 5, 1770, an angry crowd pelted the redcoats with stones and ice balls. The redcoats shot several in the crowd, including Attucks, and argued that they had been acting in self-defense. After a trial in which John Adams served as defense counsel, the jury acquitted most of the soldiers, but convicted the one who had killed Attucks.

The stamp depicts Attucks carrying a club, a common practice of Bostonians for defense against the occupying soldiers.

The nation of Antigua also issued bicentennial stamps: “Privates, Clark’s Illinois Regiment”, “Riflemen, Pennsylvania Militia”, and “Engraved American Powder Horn.” (Antigua nos. 423, 424, 425.) India issued a beautiful reproduction of the full “Spirit of 76” painting. (The upper part of the painting is U.S. nos. 1629-31.)

Other foreign stamps depicting firearms and celebrating the U.S. bicentennial were issued by the Comoros Islands, Dominica, Equatorial Guinea, Gambia, Mexico, the Indian state of Nagaland, the Popular Republic of Congo, the Solomon Islands, Tunisia, and Upper Volta (today, the Central African Republic).
