The January 8, 1815, U.S. victory at the Battle of New Orleans was one of the  most astonishing in American history. Having defeated Napoleon in the  Peninsular Campaign in Spain, the cream of the British army was shipped across  the Atlantic Ocean, to capture the port of New Orleans. Without that port,  commerce in the western United States would be destroyed.

Led by General Andrew Jackson (on the right), a combination of professional  soldiers, militia, irregulars, free Blacks, Whites, Creoles, Cajuns, Spaniards,  Frenchmen, Portuguese, Germans, Italians, Indians, lawyers, privateers, farmers,  and shopkeepers confronted the best units of the best army in the world. The  outnumbered Americans, firing their rifles from improvised fortifications,  demolished the British while suffering hardly any casualties themselves. The  Tennessee militia arrived in raccoon caps, and the Kentucky militia was clad in  rags. Militiamen brought their own hunting guns, because the federal  government lacked the resources to supply them with standardized military  weapons. Many were experienced hunters and skilled marksmen.

![American Pennsylvania-Kentucky flintlock long rifle, circa 1795. Features an extra-long, full-length plain maple wood stock, a heavy dark metal barrel, brass furniture hardware, and a double-set trigger system](../img/itemimgs/pennsylvania_kentucky_flintlock_long_rifle.jpg)

While the American guns at New Orleans were very diverse, the most common  were Pennsylvania-Kentucky rifles. The photo shows a [Pennsylvania-Kentucky Rifle from 1795](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/a-prospering-new-republic-1780-to-1860/case-8-romance-of-the-long-rifle/pennsylvania-kentucky-flintlock-long-rifle.aspx).

Soon, the young nation had a new #1 hit song, “The Hunters of Kentucky.” It  exulted: “For Jackson he was wide awake, he was not scared of trifles. Full well  he knew what aim we’d take with our Kentucky rifles.”
