The first battle of the Civil War took place in Virginia, not far  from Washington, D.C. Supporters of the Union were so  confident that the professional U.S. Army would quickly smash  the rebels that they brought picnics to watch the battle. The  surprising Confederate victory ended the notion that the  war would be over quickly.

![Civil War-era U.S. Springfield Model 1855 percussion rifle-musket. The long infantry arm features a full-length oil-finished walnut stock secured by three iron barrel bands, a long polished steel barrel in a "bright" finish, a distinctive raised hump on the lockplate containing the Maynard tape-primer door beneath the hammer, and an underlying steel ramrod.](../img/itemimgs/u_s_springfield_model_1855_rifle_musket.jpg)

This [1855 Springfield Rifle Musket](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/a-nation-asunder-1861-to-1865/case-15-union-muskets-and-rifles/us-springfield-model-1855-rifle-musket.aspx) was used by Private  Sheldon Harran of the 2nd Vermont Infantry at the First  Battle of Bull Run. (Which is usually called “Manassas” in  Southern sources.)

Many Confederate soldiers had grown up on farms and  were familiar with firearms. Often, they brought their own  guns to service. In contrast, many Union soldiers were  urbanites, including recent immigrants from places like  Ireland, where ordinary people were not allowed to own  guns. The Union’s firearms training of new recruits was  minimal. Throughout the war, the Confederates had the  advantage of much greater firearms proficiency among their soldiers.
