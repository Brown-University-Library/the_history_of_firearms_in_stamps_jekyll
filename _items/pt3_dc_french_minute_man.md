This is another 1925 stamp honoring the 150th anniversary of the battle of Lexington & Concord. The borders of this stamp and of the other 1925 stamps (Scott Numbers 617 and 618) are identical, including the crossed muskets and hanging powderhorns on the left and right sides.

When the Concord Monument was dedicated in 1826, poet Ralph Waldo introduced his new poem, the [Concord Hymn](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/45870/concord-hymn), It begins with the words reprinted on the stamp:

> By the rude bridge that arched the flood,
>
> Their flag to April’s breeze unfurled
>
> Here once the embattled farmers stood
>
> And fired the shot heard round the world.

Emerson’s father, Rev. William Emerson, had fought at the Concord Bridge that day, where the American militia turned back the British advance.

On the stamp, the pedestal is emblazoned with “The Minute Men.” They were a subset of the militia who had volunteered to be first responders. As the name indicated, they had to be ready to fight in a minute’s notice—having all their equipment ready and their guns loaded.
