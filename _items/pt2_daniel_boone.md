This is the only American stamp that depicts a firearm without the user.

Of course Daniel Boone carried an American Long Rifle as he explored Tennessee and beyond in the late 18th and early 19th centuries. Thanks in part of the long barrel, American Long Rifles were outstandingly accurate at a distance. A good hunter could “bark” a squirrel off a tree--shooting the tail so that the squirrel fell to the ground, with meat intact.

At close quarters, a musket could be used as club, but the American Long Rifle was too delicate for that purpose. So almost everyone who carried an American Long Rifle also carried an additional weapon, such as a tomahawk, for close quarters fighting. For persons who carried muskets, which were sturdier than long rifles, the close quarters weapon was often a bayonet affixed below the musket’s muzzle.

The powder horn, on the lower left, was made from a hollowed out animal horn, and used to carry gunpowder. On the right tip of the powder horn is a charger, for dispensing an exact quantity of gunpowder into a firearm when reloading.
