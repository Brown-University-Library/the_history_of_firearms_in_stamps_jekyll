Most of the Pilgrims brought the most common affordable  arm of their time, a matchlock musket. The photo shows a [typical English musket](https://www.nramuseum.org/the-museum/the-galleries/ancient-firearms/case-1-the-handcannon-the-matchlock/english-matchlock-musket.aspx). The musket was a heavy long gun, not known for accuracy or distance.

![Antique 17th-century English matchlock musket with a dark wood stock, a long metal barrel, and a flared fish-tail butt. A close-up view focuses on the side plate's matchlock mechanism, including the S-shaped serpentine match holder, flash pan, and trigger.](../img/itemimgs/english_matchlock_musket.jpg)

A matchlock was ignited by the user pressing the trigger to  lower a slow-burning hemp cord to the firing pan, thus  igniting the gunpowder. Matchlocks were adequate for  European-style battles, where rows of soldiers fired  without aiming at the mass of enemy soldiers across an  open field. But matchlocks were ill-suited for hunting or for fighting in the American woods, because the burning cord  revealed the user’s location and was prone to failure in  rainy or damp conditions. So the American colonists quickly  switched from matchlocks to more expensive flintlocks  with more reliable ignition.
