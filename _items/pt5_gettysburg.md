"The third and final day of the Battle of Gettysburg was the  high water mark of the Confederacy. If Pickett’s Charge had  broken the Union line, southeastern Pennsylvania would  have fallen, France or the United Kingdom might have  granted diplomatic recognition to the Confederacy, and the Union might have had to sue for peace. Or at least General  Robert E. Lee hoped so. 

This stamp depicts the brother against brother nature of the  Civil War. The Union soldier, with a blue background,  struggles with the Confederate soldier, on the gray  background. They are so close that they cannot use their  bayonets. 

![U.S. Harpers Ferry Model 1841 Mississippi percussion rifle, featuring a dark wood stock, a round steel barrel secured by two bright brass bands, a brass trigger guard, and a distinctive brass patchbox on the buttstock](../img/itemimgs/u_s_harpers_ferry_model_1841_mississippi_percussion_rifle.webp)

The Confederate soldier perhaps holds an [1841 Harpers  Ferry percussion rifle](https://www.nramuseum.org/guns/the-galleries/a-prospering-new-republic-1780-to-1860/case-11-firearms-innovations/us-harpers-ferry-model-1841-mississippi-percussion-rifle.aspx), popularly known as the Mississippi  Rifle. This standard issue rifle first saw service in the  Mexican War, and was used by both sides in the Civil War.  This particular model was manufactured in 1851\. On the top  left, most of the rifle’s rear sight is visible. The hammer is in  the down position, after striking a percussion cap."
